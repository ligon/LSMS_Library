The Ghana Living Standards Survey (Ghana LSS) is one of two major surveys we have for Ghana, the other being the Ghana Social Panel Survey (GhanaSPS).

* Survey Program

The GLSS is conducted by the Ghana Statistical Service (GSS).  Seven
rounds span 1987--2017.  Each round is designed as an independent
nationally representative cross-section; there is no rotating panel
or refreshment sample mechanism.

* Sampling Design

** Overview

Two-stage stratified cluster sample.  Enumeration Areas (EAs) from
the national census serve as the PSU, with a fixed take of
households per EA.

** Strata

The stratification scheme evolved across rounds:

| Rounds      | Strata definition                                     | Count |
|-------------+-------------------------------------------------------+-------|
| GLSS1--GLSS2 | Ecological zone (Coastal, Forest, Savannah)          |     9 |
|             | x Urban classification (Urban, Semi-urban, Rural)     |       |
| GLSS3+      | Region-based (10 administrative regions from GLSS3;   | ~10   |
|             | possibly finer in later rounds)                       |       |

** Weights

GLSS1 and GLSS2 are *self-weighting* (equal probability of selection;
no weight variable in the data).  GLSS3 (1991-92) has a =weight=
variable in =POV_GH.DTA= but it is constant 1.0 (self-weighting).
GLSS4 (1998-99) =POV_GH.DTA= has probability weights normalised
to mean ~1 (sum ~ n), which are a different type from the expansion
weights in later rounds.

GLSS5 (2005-06) onward provides *expansion weights* in the cover
page or location file: =weight= in =sec0.dta= (GLSS5), =weight= in
=g6loc_edt.dta= (GLSS6), =WTA_S= in =g7sec0.dta= (GLSS7).  These
sum to the national population.

The =sample= feature only includes expansion weights (GLSS5--GLSS7)
to avoid mixing weight types.  GLSS1--GLSS4 have =weight= and
=panel_weight= as NaN.

Since every round is an independent cross-section, there is no
panel weight.  =panel_weight= equals =weight= for all waves.

=clust= in all rounds, but the numbering scheme changes:

| Rounds    | Cluster ID range | Notes                             |
|-----------+------------------+-----------------------------------|
| GLSS1     | 1xxx             | 200 EAs from 1984 census frame    |
| GLSS2     | 2xxx             | 200 EAs, fresh draw               |

The changing prefix makes cross-wave cluster linkage impossible

* Panel Linkage (GLSS1 to GLSS2)

Despite being designed as independent cross-sections, a sub-sample
of GLSS1 households was re-interviewed in GLSS2.  The linkage is in
=PANELC.DAT= (present in both the 1987-88 and 1988-89 Data
directories), mapping =HID1= (GLSS1) to =HID2= (GLSS2) at the
person level.  Deduplicating to household level yields 714 panel
households.

No cross-wave linkage exists for GLSS3 onward --- cluster numbering
schemes changed between rounds and no prior-wave household ID
columns are available.  The =reint= variable in GLSS6 flags 1,476
"re-interview" households but provides no prior-wave ID for matching.

The =panel_ids= feature in =ghanalss.py= handles the GLSS1--GLSS2
linkage via the =Waves= dictionary.

Data on expenditures is somewhat unusual, in two different ways.

First, for waves before 2016--17, the survey instrument elicits the amount spent in purchasing different items, but does /not/ elicit quantities purchased.  (A separate price survey was conducted, and could presumably be used to obtain quantities.)  A separate module elicits quantities of home produced food consumed, as well as the hypothetical price at which home produced goods could be sold.

Second, starting in the 1991--92 wave, rather than eliciting expenditures over a single recall period, repeated visits (about a dozen in all) are made over the course of about a month, with expenditures and consumption from own production collected at every visit.  In =food_acquired= we sum over these visits (but it might be interesting to examine patterns at the visit level).

NB: Differences in elicitation across waves may complicate comparisons across those waves.  In particular, the two rounds in the 1980s ask for expenditures "since my last visit", but only for one visit, while subsequent rounds elicit this information across several visits.

#+begin_src python
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import cfe.regression as rgsn

z = pd.read_parquet('../var/household_characteristics.parquet')
x = pd.read_parquet('../var/food_expenditures.parquet')
o = pd.read_parquet('../2016-17/_/other_features.parquet')

z = z.droplevel('m').join(o)
z = z.iloc[:,4:]  # Drop boys, girls, men, women

x = x.join(z)['purchased_value']

y = np.log(x.replace(0,np.nan)).dropna()

y = y.reorder_levels(['j','t','m','i'])
y.index.names = ['i','t','m','j']

z.index.names = ['i','t','m']

r = rgsn.Regression(y=y,d=z)

r.predicted_expenditures()
r.get_gamma_se()

r.to_pickle('../var/cfe_demands.rgsn')

* Features NOT available in GhanaLSS

** Shocks
The GLSS (Ghana Living Standards Survey) does not include a household shocks module in any wave (GLSS1 through GLSS7, covering 1987--2017).  Unlike the LSMS-ISA surveys (Uganda, Malawi, Tanzania, Ethiopia, Nigeria), the GLSS questionnaire has no section asking households whether they experienced specific shocks, the effect on income/assets/production/consumption, or coping strategies employed.

The only tangentially related content is in GLSS7 Section 13E (governance module), which asks three perception questions about whether flooding, health hazards, and natural disasters are "threats of concern."  These are subjective perception questions, not experienced-shock data, and cannot be mapped to the standard =shocks= schema.

Ghana's LSMS-ISA participation is through the GhanaSPS (Social Panel Survey), not the GLSS.  Shocks data for Ghana should be sought in GhanaSPS if available.
